Philosphy & Values


I structure my life around John Stuart Mill's Harm Principle. I believe in the freedom of action so long as that action does not deprive another of their freedom of action. That is the gist of it. There is much more nuance and necessary distinctions between "harm" and "offence", but this is not the place to explain the theory, nor could I articulate the theory better than JS Mill on the first paragraph of the Wikipedia page.

Planning life:

Unless one lives an exhaustingly boring life, I do not see the point in planning more than 6 months ahead. Exceptions being finances and a few other specific goals, not much can be foretold about the future. You could move across the country, find new friend, have a great job application accepted, find the love of your life, lose your job, discover that your partner had an affair, be a victim of a traumatic event, or be diagnosed with a debilitating disease. There are plenty of ways that your life could be turned upsidedown, for better or worse. At best. pre-planning your life will cause dissapointment as your plans fall short. At worst, pre-planning your life will cause you to ignore spontaneous opportunities that could have changed your life for the better AND cause dissapointment as your plans fall short.


If present day Gray relies on plans that future day Gray must achieve to avoid a problematic situation, then Gray is at a signifcant liability of an uncertain future. The same line of reasoning applies to anyone. If I take out $300,000 of student loans now and rely on future me to pay back those loans, then what happens if I flunk out of college, or the job compensation is not as lucrative as I expected? Then I owe $300,000 and interest, and I don't have a feasiable way of paying it off. To put it kindly, if you are in that sort of situation, you fucked up. You relied on future you to accept the risk of past you, and the gamble did not go in your favor. Its a trap that many people fall into, and with the absolutely garbage social welfare saftey net in the United States, it is essentially a life sentence.


Now, when I say that planning for the future is pointless, I do not mean that planning for a future is pointless. You may be asking, what is the difference between the future and a future? Let me break it down.


the future: is the inevitable course that time will take between now and a date that has not yet occured. the future is unknown, and we can only reflect on it after is has occured.


a future: is one of the possible courses that the future could take. It is unknown with certainty which one of the many courses that the true future could take. a is simply a possibility. It is not a guarentee that it will or will not happen.


Planning for the future will backfire. You cannot predict the future


Planning for a future is smart. You can plan and coordinate your actions ahead of time incase a situation takes place. Have you ever participated in a fire-drill at school? The purpose of the drill is to plan for a future in which there is a fire. it does NOT predict that there WILL be a fire.


You can plan for a job loss by saving money in a emergency fund. You can plan for a unexpected snow storm by keeping extra warm clothes and batteries. You can plan for a medical catastrophe by having comphrensive insurance. None of these potential futures are optimal, but you can plan for the event that a possible negative future could become the future. You cannot plan without signifcant risk that a future you want will become the future.
